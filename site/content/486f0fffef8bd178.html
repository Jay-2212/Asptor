Alexander Zverev’s Grand Slam win puts Type 1 diabetes in the spotlight

• THE HINDU NATIONAL • 2026-07-17

On June 7, a five-set epic on the Roland Garros clay ended with Germany’s Alexander Zverev winning his first Grand Slam title at the 2026 French Open. He also became, on his thirty-fourth attempt, the first German to win a major men’s singles title in three decades. He reached the finals stage again at the Wimbledon championships on July 12, 2026. And perhaps most of all, Zverev became the first person with type I diabetes (T1D) to triumph in this contest.

While he is far from the first elite athlete to live with T1D, his French Open win is an important achievement. T1D is a strenuous condition to manage for a layperson, let alone a sportsperson. Zverev himself has called the process of managing blood glucose levels during a tennis set as “playing two games at once”.

Understanding T1D

Like most people with the condition, Zverev was diagnosed with T1D when he was four years old.

T1D is an autoimmune disease that affects the pancreas by destroying its beta-cells, which produce insulin. This is different from type II diabetes, where the body can produce insulin but resists its effects. People with T1D need to take insulin externally to use sugar from food to produce energy.

As the body digests sugars and carbohydrates in food, it breaks them down into glucose, the main fuel for the body’s cells. After a meal, blood glucose levels rise, triggering the release of insulin. Insulin helps absorb glucose from the bloodstream for immediate use or store excess glycogen for later use.

Without enough insulin, or when the body can’t use it effectively, glucose builds up in the bloodstream, causing hyperglycaemia, or high blood glucose. In the short term, this can lead to increased thirst, frequent urination, headache, blurred vision, and fatigue. Over time, persistently high blood glucose can damage the eyes, kidneys, and other organs.

Managing highs

Tennis is both praised and scorned for its unique and dynamic scoring system: it barely has room for luck yet lends itself to soaring comebacks. A game in which a player leads 40-love can suddenly swing to deuce, which could lead to a long tussle for the advantage point. A player who appears to be cruising towards a 6-3 set win can suddenly find themselves locked in four additional games and a tiebreak.

Concurrently, hyperglycaemia causes energy levels in the body to fluctuate. This is usually managed by injecting insulin into the fat tissue under the skin, wherefrom it is absorbed into the bloodstream and carried around the body. Devices to do this include insulin pens, syringes, and insulin pumps.

The insulin pen contains cartridges with the hormone. A user can set the number of units insulin required and push on the injection button. With enough practice, it can be done discreetly in public places as well. The American Diabetes Association (ADA) has advised pen users to administer the shot in their abdomen.

However, a lack of awareness has led to misconceptions about injecting insulin. Zverev faced such an instance at the 2023 French Open, where a match official told him he could not inject insulin on court because it looked “weird” and that he would have to use his three-minute toilet break for that.

“I said, ‘What does it look like? That I’m doping?’ This argument makes no sense,” he told Eurosport Germany. He added that his bathroom break was not enough and that he generally has to inject around four to five times during a match.

He also caught flak in 2019 for after appearing to check a device inside his gear bag during matches, which was against the rules preventing players from communicating by phone. Germany’s Davis Cup captain Michael Kohlmann told DW-TV that Zverev was, in those cases, checking his glucose monitoring device.

Surviving the lows

On the flip side, insulin therapy carries a risk of hypoglycaemia, or low blood sugar. This can lead to loss of consciousness, cardiac arrest or even death if it isn’t dealt with. Dealing requires ingesting glucose tablets or eating fast-acting carbohydrates and sugars in the form of juices, sodas or candies.

However, fluctuating sugar levels don’t always show symptoms until it is too late, and generally result in loss of consciousness at first.

According to the ADA, 40% of T1D patients are unaware of hypoglycaemia. People with T1D are known to manage the rally between high and low blood sugar with trial and error.

While patients have generally used finger-prick blood tests or glucometers, continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) devices have become commonplace, and offer more accurate, real-time updates, per the ADA.

CGMs have a sensor in the form of a small piece of material inserted under the patient’s skin using a needle. The sensor transmits real-time glucose levels (via a transmitter) to a smartphone app, receiver or insulin pump. A simple computer on the smartphone or the pump decides how much insulin should be injected, how much carbohydrates to consume, etc.

More than a physical burden

Players like Zverev also have to deal with the uncertainty of when a hyper- or hypoglycemic episode could kick in during a match. A 2025 study by researchers at the University of Georgia School of Social Work found that the hypervigilance required to manage T1D often causes burnout and exacerbates mental illnesses.

Hypoglycaemia can also impair sleep efficiency and quality, which are important for sportspersons’ mental and physical upkeep. Tennis tournaments, especially the majors, have multiple matches in a week and one-day gaps between matches, which means only small recovery periods.

Recovery nutrition in tennis focuses heavily on restoring glycogen levels through carbohydrates. However, the unpredictable nature of the condition also means there’s a risk of a glucose spike, which could leave the athlete without being able to consume the necessary recovery carbohydrates. The physical exertion in a match could later crash the glucose levels, and render a sleepless night.

Type 1 on the map

Zverev has said he was told he could never become an elite athlete after his T1D diagnosis. He was diagnosed at age four, spent years of his career hiding the condition, and continues to manage it in discreet ways.

“When something unexpected happens, I’ve learned to stay calm and trust the systems I have in place,” he told Reuters in January this year. Between points and changeovers, he has said he finds himself calculating when next to take a sip from his bottle or have an energy gel.

Among others, he uses CGMs to help regulate his glucose levels. They have been his tools of choice, although his Halle Open match on June 21, which he lost, was also a bump: a malfunctioning glucose monitor gave him an incorrect reading and he injected more insulin than he needed.

He has also advocated for people with T1D both on and off the court, and set up a foundation in his name to support children with types I and II diabetes.

While he has been having a positive impact on how people live with T1D, Zverev’s legacy off the court has been chequered.

He settled a legal dispute in 2025 over allegations of domestic violence (which he has denied). The Association of Tennis Professionals also closed an investigation in 2023, stemming from allegations of physical abuse by former tennis player Olya Sharypova, citing insufficient evidence.
