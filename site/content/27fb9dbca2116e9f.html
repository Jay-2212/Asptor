Does national recognition come belatedly for south Indian artistes?

• THE HINDU OPINION • 2026-07-17

Legendary playback singer S. Janaki, who passed away on July 11, 2026, had declined the Padma Bhushan, arguing that the honour had come far too late in her career. She maintained that artists should be recognised when they are at the height of their creative powers rather than in their old age or after death. A similar debate has long surrounded the Jnanpith Award as well, with critics arguing that several outstanding writers from south India have been overlooked despite their enduring contributions to Indian literature. Are these concerns justified? Does national recognition come belatedly for south Indian artistes? T.M. Krishna and A.R. Venkatachalapathy discuss this question in a conversation moderated by B. Kolappan.

Do you think there is some bias in the selection process? Are people from north India favoured when it comes to recipients?

T.M. Krishna: So, the first thing we have to acknowledge is that the Padma Awards have never been apolitical. Your political affiliation has influenced these choices, and where you come from has definitely influenced them. Secondly, if elections are coming up in a particular State, we can be assured that the Padma Awards will include an extra share of recipients from that State. Whether or not that has any electoral benefit is a different question.

In the world of the performing arts, there has always been a great deal of lobbying for these awards. I know artists who have spent a lot of time in Delhi, meeting people in power in order to procure these awards. There is also an overbearing presence of — again, when we say north India, we have to be very careful — circles concentrated, I would rather say in Mumbai and Delhi. The cultures centred around these two cities have an overwhelming influence on the conferring of these awards. Another issue is the conferring of inappropriate honours. People who have devoted their entire lives to their art receive a Padma Shri, whereas others who are younger and whose achievements do not compare receive a Padma Bhushan.

We are talking about the south. We do not talk about the Northeast. Just think about how many artists from the Northeast receive these awards. It is also a question of which art forms are recognised and how they are chosen. What is the process?

There is another set of awards, the Sangeet Natak Akademi Awards. I think the Sahitya Akademi, at least for a long time, had some degree of independence. The Sangeet Natak Akademi Awards have never even had that level of independence. They have also been influenced by political affiliation and by whoever is in power. The fact that a few good decisions have been made does not mean the system is sound. Fundamentally, it gives an unfair advantage to people who are closer to the centres of power in Delhi.

A.R. Venkatachalapathy: Sivaji Ganesan never received the National Award for Best Actor. Instead, adding insult to injury, the great artist was given the Best Supporting Actor award. Similarly, M.S. Viswanathan never received a National Award for his near-immortal music. But there is a larger systemic issue that needs to be addressed. Sports and cinema have a disproportionate hold over these awards. Even in the case of S. Janaki, I was not very happy when she refused the Padma Bhushan. There are so many writers and poets who have never been recognised. They would be delighted to receive even a Padma Shri in their 90s. Disproportionate importance is given to sportspeople and film personalities. Third- and fourth-rung cinema stars receive awards, while many of the country’s finest artists and writers are overlooked.

M.G. Ramachandran (MGR) was awarded the Bharat Ratna, while many people who made outstanding contributions to India’s economy and public life were recognised much later. Do you think the conferring of the Bharat Ratna is influenced by politics?

A.R. Venkatachalapathy: Every award is accompanied by politics. Whether it is the Bharat Ratna being conferred on MGR or the Chennai Central railway station being renamed as Puratchi Thalaivar Dr M.G. Ramachandran Central Railway Station — all these are political decisions. Even when the personalities being honoured are truly deserving, if such decisions are taken at particular moments for immediate political gain, there should be pushback. We should be critical of such timing. Sachin Tendulkar is a Bharat Ratna. But we find him endorsing commercial products. There is perhaps nothing legally wrong, but I think it is morally inappropriate. A Bharat Ratna has been conferred on figures such as Dr. Ambedkar, Rajaji, Nehru and Bismillah Khan. It carries a certain gravitas.

T.M. Krishna: There is no question that Sachin Tendulkar deserved the honour. But think about the timing. If I remember correctly, it was around his final Test match. You should not time a Bharat Ratna to coincide with a public event. In my opinion, that is unethical. We can equally ask: where are Sunil Gavaskar and Kapil Dev? They too deserve the honour. But when you time it as a public spectacle, you diminish the award itself. I agree with Mr.Venkatachalapathy that the system is the problem.

A.R. Venkatachalapathy: I also think that posthumous awards are acceptable. After all, we can make mistakes, and we do not know when someone is going to pass away. But what constitutes a posthumous award? Can we confer the Bharat Ratna on Thiruvalluvar, Avvaiyar, Vidyapati or Tansen? There should be a time limit from the date of a person’s death. Perhaps within a year or two after the death of the candidates.

T.M. Krishna: The Bharat Ratna is not just about excellence. It is also about an individual’s contribution to, and presence in, the larger cultural life of the country. That distinction is important. Which is why every great artist need not necessarily be awarded a Bharat Ratna. M.S. Subbulakshmi is a classic example. She transcended every boundary imaginable. Amartya Sen, as an economist, similarly transcended national boundaries. They represent India not only nationally but internationally.

But do you think many musicians are overlooked when it comes to the other Padma Awards?

A.R. Venkatachalapathy: I would say that the classical arts have received disproportionate recognition when it comes to the Padma awards. But if you look at other fields, particularly Tamil literature, the picture is different. Apart from Jayakanthan, who received recognition in his prime, the most respected historian from Tamil Nadu today is Y. Subbarayalu. He is 86 years old. His knowledge of Chola history and inscriptions is unmatched. But has he been recognised by the Government of Tamil Nadu? Has he even received a Kalaimamani [the highest civilian arts award conferred by Tamil Nadu]?

T.M. Krishna: Kalaimamani is perhaps the worst example of how an award can be conferred. There have long been rumours that it can be bought. Recipients are simply lined up and presented with awards, sometimes several years’ worth in a single ceremony. The State government must be held responsible. It has allowed a State award to become insignificant.

A.R. Venkatachalapathy: I agree. All awards should be conferred through a transparent process. The Government of Tamil Nadu confers numerous awards at the time of Pongal. If you look at the list of previous recipients, it is a very mixed bag and it is not entirely clear if any real standard has been applied. There are many problems. But one has to begin by getting the process right.

T.M. Krishna: Whether it is the State Government or the Central Government, we do not value the Ministry of Culture. So, what is its job? What does it actually do? During the COVID-19 pandemic, through our foundation, we worked to support artists in various districts of Tamil Nadu. The Government of Tamil Nadu does not even maintain a registry of the known troupes representing different art forms across the State. Artists from marginalised communities and practitioners of diverse art forms have no meaningful support system. If the government is not engaging with the cultural life of the State or with its artists, it cannot claim to know them.

A.R. Venkatachalapathy: We may have our criticisms of the Sahitya Akademi or the Lalit Kala Akademi but, compared with State-level institutions, they have generally performed better. The Sahitya Akademi, the Lalit Kala Akademi and other such institutions were created on the basis of an autonomous model. Although they were funded by the government, they enjoyed a considerable degree of institutional autonomy. They have failed on many counts, but they have also succeeded in many respects.

Listen to the conversation

T.M. Krishna is an acclaimed Carnatic vocalist and recipient of the Ramon Magsaysay Award; A.R. Venkatachalapathy is an historian and Sahitya Akademi Award-winning scholar.
