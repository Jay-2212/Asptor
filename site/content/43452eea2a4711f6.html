Tale behind the change of a government department’s name in Tamil Nadu

• THE HINDU NATIONAL • 2026-07-15

The recent change, carried out by the Tamilaga Vettri Kazhagam (TVK)-led government, in the nomenclature of the Adi Dravidar and Tribal Welfare Department as the Social Justice Department in Tamil Nadu has a fairly long history.

Veteran civil servant Vishwanath A. Shegaonkar, who was Secretary of the Adi-Dravidar and Tribal Welfare department in the later part of the DMK regime of 2006-11, had told this correspondent then that the proposal to change the name of the department was the only file of his department which had eluded a solution from the Cabinet.

For nearly 25 years, the Puthiya Tamilagam (PT) president K. Krishnasamy had been carrying on a campaign against the Tamil nomenclature of the Department for the Welfare of Adi Dravidar and Tribes. He had unsuccessfully raised the issue in the Assembly twice - the first time in January 2001 and the second in September 2011, when the then Chief Ministers Karunanidhi and Jayalalithaa respectively were present in the House.

Puthiya Tamilagam founder K. Krishnasamy | Photo Credit: L. Balachandar

Making his point under the broad subject of ‘Dalit identity’, the Puthiya Tamilagam leader said Adi Dravidars were only a section of those included in the list of Scheduled Castes (SC). This was because of the inclusion of 70-odd castes effectively enumerated under the list of SCs in Tamil Nadu. As all sections wanted their own identity, it would only be proper to rename the department as SC Welfare department, he pointed out.

He sought a literal translation of the term — Scheduled Castes — and suggested that the people be called “pattiyal ina makkal” and the department be called “pattiyal ina nalathurai,” according to a report of The Hindu on February 1, 2001. However, Chief Minister Karunanidhi, turned down the request. Noting that the SCs were earlier known as Harijans, he recalled the demand of the Dravidar Kazhagam founder E.V. Ramasamy for renaming as Adi Dravidars. Only during the period of M.G. Ramachandran as Chief Minister was the demand conceded. The name-change was effected after “consultation with all of us,” Karunanidhi recalled, adding it would not be proper to change the name again.

When Dr. Krishnasamy raised the matter again 10 years later, Jayalalithaa chose not to react. But, it was left to C.K. Thamizharasan (Republican Party of India) to counter the PT founder, saying the existing name was generic in nature and the issue was discussed even in 1920s, stated in a report of this newspaper on September 6, 2011. Both the PT and the RPI were allies of the AIADMK then.

Republican Party of India state president C.K. Thamizharasan (centre) at Vellore in May 2011 | Photo Credit: D. Gopalakrishnan

Dr. Krishnasamy, almost one-and-a-half years later, cited an order of the Union government issued on November 22, 2012, asking all the State governments to use the exact translation of SC in regional languages. He also mentioned how the Centre, in 1982, issued an order that the word ‘Harijan’ be dropped in all official communication.

In November 2019, the then AIADMK government, led by Edappadi K. Palaniswami, informed the Madras High Court that a committee chaired by the then Adi Dravidar Welfare Minister [V.M. Rajalakshmi] had decided there was no need for changing the name of the department and making it a literal translation of the terms “Scheduled Castes” and “Scheduled Tribes” in Tamil. Its response came when a public interest litigation petition filed by S. Marimuthu, a person belonging to Devendra Kula Vellalar community, for changing the name of the department was listed for admission. The petitioner’s submission was that the department could be simply called the Social Welfare department.

Rettamalai Srinivasan. File | Photo Credit: Special Arrangement

Those who were against the name change had cited how tall leaders of the community in the British era — M.C. Rajah and Rettamalai Srinivasan — had fought hard to get the recognition for the term, ‘Adi-Dravida.’

In January 1922, Rajah, while moving a resolution in the then Legislative Council for the change in the nomenclature of the Depressed Classes, argued that ‘Paraiya’ and ‘Panchama’, the terms denoting the depressed classes were nothing but the references to call them the “outcaste people.” He got support from his colleagues in the House, Thaticonda Namberumal Chetty, M.C. Madurai Pillai and K. Srinivasa Aiyangar. On January 20, 1922, the motion was carried. Two months later, the Law department issued an order, according to “Adi Dravidar Varalaru” authored A. Perumal Pillai.

Former Union Minister Murasoli Maran, in his work, Dravida Iyakka Varalaru (volume I), stated that even during the 1921 Census, 15,025 persons had identified their caste as ‘Adi Dravidar.’ One of the founding leaders of the Justice Party, C. Natesa Mudaliar, had earlier introduced a motion before the Council of the then Madras Municipal Corporation that ‘Panchamars’ be known as ‘Adi-Dravidars,’ Maran wrote.

Murasoli Maran | Photo Credit: The Hindu Archives

Given this background, Viduthalai Chiruthaigal Katchi (VCK)’s Member of Parliament (Villupuram) D. Ravikumar, former legislator M. Jagan Moorthy and former member of the State government’s panel to monitor the practice of social justice in government departments S. Devadoss have not accepted the change. Though not against the creation of a department for social justice, their stand is that the original identity of the department should not be lost and the term, ‘Adi Dravidar,’ does not refer to any particular social denomination.

However, the incumbent Social Justice Minister Vanni Arasu, who is also from the VCK, has defended the government’s move, which, according to him, is aimed at reflecting a “broader vision.” It remains to be seen whether future governments will stick to the current nomenclature or will change it.
